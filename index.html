April 19, 2026

Mythos, Muse, and the Opportunity Cost of Compute

Ben Thompson · Stratechery

Thompson argues that opportunity cost, not marginal cost, is the binding constraint for the hyperscalers and frontier labs. The environment now forces sharp trade-offs about which customers to serve and which workloads to prioritize, and the bifurcation between strong and weak CEOs widens as a result. A useful lens for thinking about where pricing power and product judgment will actually compound.

AI Populism's Warning Shots

Jasmine Sun · Substack

Sun frames AI populism as a worldview in which AI is not a normal technology but an elite political project to be resisted. The claim worth taking seriously is that caring about AI is decoupling from knowing about AI, which means policy is about to be shaped by coalitions with preexisting agendas rather than technical understanding. Could well be the defining political story of the next year.

The Fellowship: The Literary Lives of the Inklings

Carol Zaleski & Philip Zaleski · Book

A group biography of Tolkien, Lewis, Barfield, and Williams that makes the case for the small-group-that-changes-the-world as an engine of cultural renewal. It is interesting to observe the ripple effects to this day of their deliberate revival of fantasy and fairy tale.

April 12, 2026

No "New Deal" for OpenAI

Will Manidis · Minutes

Manidis dismantles OpenAI's "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" by pointing out what the framing conveniently omits: the original New Deal was not a workshop between capital and labor but a settlement forced by decades of industrial violence and the credible threat of more. The piece stands out as one of the clearest arguments for why AI's current window of voluntary concessions will close, and once it does, the relationship between industry and public becomes permanently adversarial.

April 5, 2026

The Art of Spending Money

Morgan Housel · Book

A worthy follow-up to The Psychology of Money. Housel's central claim is that spending well is an intensely personal act, not a quantitative optimization problem. The most useful distinction: spending for independence compounds in ways that spending for social comparison never can.

If You're Not First, You're (Maybe? Potentially? Probably?) Last

0xsmac · Semi-Lucid Thoughts

A precise framework for why the "catch-up trade" in emerging categories almost always fails. The mechanisms that drove the leader to its position compound more aggressively when the market accelerates, and the right tail outcome overwhelmingly favors whoever is architecting for it. The real investor challenge: identifying what must change for the secondary player to become the beneficiary.

March 29, 2026

The Techno-Solutionist Commitment

Kyle Harrison · Investing 101

Solutionism as a commitment rather than a temperament. Harrison makes the case that neither blind optimism nor doomerism is sufficient. The obligation is to identify problems clearly and engineer targeted solutions. Progress requires active participation, not passive expectation that things will improve on their own.

Get in the Token Path

Jamin Ball · Clouded Judgement

A thought-provoking framework for where AI business models are heading. The core claim is that tokens are becoming the consumption primitive of the AI era the same way compute cycles were for cloud. The credit abstraction layer is the most interesting angle: companies can manage shifting model complexity underneath without exposing it to customers, selling outcomes rather than raw compute.

Anthropic Mythos and the Next Model Leap

Andrew Curran · X

A compelling synthesis of the Anthropic Mythos rumors and their second-order implications. The most provocative claim: if very large training runs become essential to staying competitive, frontier intelligence may become too expensive for most of humanity to afford. The strategic implications for compute, energy, and the widening gap between frontier labs and everyone else are worth watching closely.

Hardball

George Stalk & Rob Lachenauer · Book

A case for relentlessness in competitive strategy. The central argument is that competitive advantage becomes decisive advantage only through sustained, aggressive execution. Some of the case studies feel dated, but the underlying framework of turning advantages into insurmountable leads remains relevant.

March 22, 2026

World Models: Computing the Uncomputable

Packy McCormick & Pim De Witte · Not Boring

An ambitious deep dive into world models as the most promising path to embodied AI. The core insight is that these models solve the compression problem the same way biology does, by learning to ignore what doesn't matter. The connections to agent-based systems from complexity science are interesting to consider.

Agents Over Bubbles

Ben Thompson · Stratechery

Thompson lays out the importance of harnesses within AI products today. The integration between model and harness is where true agent differentiation is found, and pairing the two could be the mechanism through which model companies capture significantly more of the value chain.

The Merchant in the Statehouse

Will Manidis · Minutes

A provocative reframing of American political economy as fundamentally Dutch, not English. State and commerce have been intertwined by design since the founding. The most striking claim: the laissez-faire era from roughly 1980 to 2020 is the historical anomaly with libertarianism as the exception, not the rule.
